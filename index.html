Foundations

Understanding obesity

Obesity is a chronic disease shaped by biology, daily life, stress, environment, and access to care. It is not just about willpower.

What is obesity?

Obesity is a chronic disease in which excess body fat can affect health and raise the risk of problems involving the heart, metabolism, breathing, joints, and mental health.

BMI is a common screening tool. It is useful, but it should be looked at along with waist size, body composition, other health conditions, and the full clinical picture.

How obesity affects health

❤

Heart and blood vessels

Raises risk of high blood pressure, heart disease, heart failure, and stroke.

🍎

Metabolism

Can contribute to insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, high cholesterol, and fatty liver disease.

🫁

Breathing

Can make breathing harder and is linked to obstructive sleep apnea.

🦽

Joints and movement

Adds stress to joints and can raise the risk of knee osteoarthritis.

🧠

Mental health

May be linked with depression, anxiety, stigma, social isolation, and lower quality of life.

🎯

Cancer risk

Is linked with a higher risk of several cancers, including colorectal and endometrial cancer.

Evidence-based care

Treatment options

Obesity treatment usually works best as long-term care. Open each option to learn what it involves, who it may help, and what to keep in mind.

Lifestyle medicine
First-line care for all patients

Lifestyle treatment is the foundation of obesity care, even when medicines or surgery are also used.

Nutrition and food choices+

No single diet is right for everyone. Eating plans work best when they are affordable, realistic, and sustainable over time.

  • Mediterranean diet — emphasizes vegetables, whole grains, olive oil, legumes, and fish. Strong evidence for heart health and metabolic benefit.
  • DASH diet — originally developed to reduce blood pressure. High in fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy.
  • Lower-carbohydrate plans — can produce faster initial weight loss. May be harder to maintain long-term.
  • Protein and fiber — both help with fullness. Prioritizing these at meals can reduce total intake without strict calorie counting.
  • Practical starting point — reducing ultra-processed food and sugary drinks often makes a meaningful difference without overhauling everything at once.
Physical activity+

Exercise improves metabolic health, mood, and cardiovascular risk even before significant weight loss occurs.

  • Aerobic activity — aim for at least 150 minutes per week of moderate intensity. Brisk walking, cycling, and swimming all count.
  • Strength training — at least twice per week helps preserve muscle mass during weight loss and improves insulin sensitivity.
  • Starting small — even 20–30 minutes of walking daily can significantly reduce cardiovascular risk.
  • Daily movement (NEAT) — standing more, taking stairs, and walking during the day adds up meaningfully.
Behavioral support+

Behavior change is one of the strongest predictors of long-term success.

  • Self-monitoring — tracking food, activity, or weight is one of the most effective single habits for weight loss.
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — helps identify patterns around emotional eating and builds strategies for lasting change.
  • Group programs — social accountability and peer support often make group-based care more effective.
  • Motivational interviewing — a patient-centered approach that helps build internal motivation for change.
Sleep+

Poor sleep directly disrupts hunger hormones and can make weight loss harder.

  • Target 7–9 hours — sleep deprivation raises ghrelin (hunger hormone) and lowers leptin (fullness hormone), increasing appetite significantly.
  • Treat sleep apnea — untreated obstructive sleep apnea interferes with weight loss and raises cardiovascular risk.
  • Consistent schedule — going to bed and waking at the same time daily helps regulate metabolic hormones.
  • Sleep environment — a cool, dark, quiet room helps. Avoiding screens before bed reduces blue light interference with melatonin.
Stress management+

Chronic stress promotes abdominal fat storage and drives emotional eating.

  • Stress and cortisol — prolonged stress keeps cortisol elevated, which increases appetite and promotes fat storage around the abdomen.
  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) — structured program with evidence for reducing emotional eating.
  • Relaxation practices — yoga, slow breathing, and progressive muscle relaxation all lower cortisol.
  • Mental health treatment — depression and anxiety are common in people with obesity. Treating them directly tends to improve weight outcomes.
Recommended as part of care for all patients
Anti-obesity medications
BMI 30+, or 27+ with a weight-related condition

Medicines can reduce hunger, improve fullness, and support meaningful weight loss when used alongside lifestyle changes.

Semaglutide (Wegovy / Ozempic)+

A GLP-1 receptor agonist that mimics a gut hormone to signal fullness, slow digestion, and reduce appetite.

  • How it works — activates GLP-1 receptors in the brain and gut, reducing appetite and slowing gastric emptying.
  • Weight loss — clinical trials show an average of around 15% total body weight loss.
  • Dosing — weekly injection, starting low and increasing over several months to reduce side effects.
  • Common side effects — nausea, vomiting, and constipation, especially during dose increases. Usually improves over time.
  • Note — Wegovy is FDA-approved for obesity; Ozempic is approved for type 2 diabetes but shares the same medicine.
Tirzepatide (Zepbound / Mounjaro)+

A dual incretin medicine that activates both GLP-1 and GIP receptors. Currently shows the strongest weight-loss results of any approved medication.

  • How it works — activates two hormone receptors at once, amplifying effects on appetite, insulin, and fat metabolism.
  • Weight loss — clinical trials show an average of around 21% total body weight loss — the highest of any approved medication so far.
  • Dosing — weekly injection, titrated from 2.5 mg up to 15 mg over several months.
  • Side effects — similar GI profile to semaglutide. Nausea is most common at dose increases.
  • Note — Zepbound is FDA-approved for obesity; Mounjaro is approved for type 2 diabetes.
Liraglutide (Saxenda)+

An older GLP-1 receptor agonist that requires daily rather than weekly injections.

  • How it works — same GLP-1 mechanism as semaglutide, but shorter-acting, requiring daily dosing.
  • Weight loss — typically around 5–8% total body weight loss in clinical studies.
  • Dosing — daily subcutaneous injection, titrated over five weeks from 0.6 mg to 3.0 mg.
  • When it may be preferred — if weekly injections are not tolerated or when familiarity with this class is a factor.
Naltrexone / bupropion (Contrave)+

An oral combination pill that acts on the brain's reward and hunger pathways. No injections required and often lower cost than injectables.

  • How it works — bupropion activates brain circuits that reduce appetite; naltrexone blocks the reward response that reinforces eating.
  • Weight loss — typically around 4–5% total body weight loss.
  • Dosing — oral tablet, twice daily, titrated over four weeks.
  • Important cautions — not for use in people with seizure disorders, uncontrolled high blood pressure, or current opioid use.
  • Advantage — oral pill, no injection required, often more affordable than injectable options.
May help some patients — eligibility depends on BMI and health history
Bariatric surgery
For severe obesity and selected patients

Surgery can be the most effective long-term treatment for severe obesity, often improving or resolving related conditions like diabetes and sleep apnea.

Sleeve gastrectomy+

The most commonly performed bariatric procedure. About 75–80% of the stomach is removed, leaving a smaller sleeve-shaped stomach.

  • How it works — smaller stomach capacity limits food intake; also reduces ghrelin, the hormone that signals hunger.
  • Weight loss — typically 25–30% of total body weight.
  • Pros — no intestinal rerouting; technically simpler than bypass; lower long-term complication rate.
  • Cons — permanent and irreversible; may worsen acid reflux in some patients.
  • Recovery — usually 1–2 days in hospital; most people return to desk work within 2–3 weeks.
Roux-en-Y gastric bypass+

A small stomach pouch is created and connected directly to the small intestine, bypassing most of the stomach and the upper portion of the intestine.

  • How it works — combines restriction with significant metabolic and hormonal changes, including a large surge in GLP-1 after meals.
  • Weight loss — typically 30–35% of total body weight — the highest of standard procedures.
  • Best for — patients with type 2 diabetes (60–80% remission rate), severe acid reflux, or those needing maximum metabolic effect.
  • Cons — more complex surgery; higher risk of dumping syndrome; lifelong vitamin and mineral supplements required.
  • Recovery — usually 2–3 days in hospital; return to desk work in 3–4 weeks.
Adjustable gastric band+

A silicone band placed around the upper stomach that can be tightened or loosened over time via a port under the skin.

  • How it works — purely restrictive; no hormonal component. The band reduces the opening to the lower stomach.
  • Weight loss — typically 15–20% of total body weight — the lowest of any bariatric option.
  • Pros — fully reversible and adjustable; lowest surgical risk; usually outpatient or one overnight stay.
  • Cons — highest long-term complication rate including band slippage and erosion. Use has declined significantly over the past decade.
What to expect before and after+

Bariatric surgery requires preparation and long-term follow-up. It is a tool, not a standalone solution.

  • Pre-surgery evaluation — typically includes medical, psychological, and nutritional assessment. Some insurers require a supervised diet program first.
  • Nutrition after surgery — vitamin and mineral supplements (B12, iron, calcium, vitamin D) are required long-term, especially after bypass.
  • Lifestyle still matters — surgery works best when combined with lasting changes in diet, activity, and follow-up care.
  • Benefits beyond weight — many patients see remission of type 2 diabetes, improvement in blood pressure, sleep apnea, and joint pain.
Most effective for severe obesity — requires full evaluation before referral
